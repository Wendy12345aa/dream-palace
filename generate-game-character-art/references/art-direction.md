## Deliverable selection

Use a single full-body 立绘 for character reveal, roster art, RPG dialogue presentation, or concept approval. Use half-body only when the target UI or composition truly calls for it. Use a turnaround for modeling or costume construction. Use a character sheet when identity, expressions, equipment, or costume details must be communicated together.

## Prompt language

Prefer concrete art-direction phrases:

- normal adult human proportions, approximately 7.5–8 heads tall
- mature game-character silhouette
- anatomically believable torso, pelvis, limbs, hands, and feet
- full-length character illustration, entire figure visible
- grounded stance with a clear center of gravity
- production character art, not mascot art

Use negative constraints sparingly but explicitly:

- no chibi, no Q-version, no super-deformed anatomy
- no oversized head, shortened limbs, tiny hands, tiny feet, or baby-like face
- no mascot, plush toy, doll, sticker, or pet styling

Avoid contradictory language. Words like cute, tiny, doll, toy, mascot, and adorable often pull the result toward chibi proportions even when followed by “normal anatomy.” Describe personality through expression, costume, palette, and gesture instead.

## Non-chibi diagnostics

Evaluate the silhouette before surface detail. A normal-proportion figure should show a readable ribcage-to-pelvis relationship, sufficient limb length, adult-sized hands and feet, and a head that does not dominate the body. Stylization may exaggerate shoulders, legs, waist, or clothing, but it must remain structurally humanoid.

Treat these as major failures:

- head visibly larger than the chosen proportion target
- shortened upper arms, forearms, thighs, or lower legs
- compressed ribcage and pelvis with a toddler-like outline
- tiny extremities used to make the character appear harmless or cute
- facial features and cranium constructed like an infant
- costume or props simplified into mascot/toy forms

## Character-sheet consistency

Keep every view at one scale and baseline. Preserve facial structure, hairstyle topology, body proportion, costume layers, asymmetry, scars, jewelry, weapon handedness, and palette. A back or side view must reveal plausible construction rather than inventing a different outfit.

Generate a coherent sheet in one pass when possible. If separate generations are necessary, use the approved neutral full-body image as the canonical reference for all later images.

## Final QA

Check the full silhouette, then the face, hands, feet, joints, costume joins, and equipment. Confirm that transparent outputs have clean edges and no unintended shadow or background residue. For sheets, compare head size, shoulder width, hip width, limb length, costume seams, and prop scale across views.
