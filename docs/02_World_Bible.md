# Chapter 2 — World Bible

> *A living kingdom is built upon a living world.*

---

# Purpose

The Project Vision explains why Dream Palace exists.

The Vision Bible explains how Dream Palace should be designed.

The World Bible defines where everything happens and how the world behaves.

Every city, faction, resource, law, technology, event, and companion responsibility should follow the rules described here.

---

# World Philosophy

The world exists to support people.

The kingdom is never merely a backdrop.

Cities should remember.

People should react.

History should matter.

The world should continue moving even when the player is absent.

Dream Palace should feel less like a map full of stages and more like a home that keeps changing.

---

# Core World Principles

## Living Kingdom

The kingdom changes every day.

Construction finishes.

Harvests grow.

Trade routes move.

Politics evolve.

Citizens react.

Companions continue working.

Nothing should feel frozen.

---

## Cause and Effect

Everything should happen for a reason.

Bad harvests affect food.

Food affects citizens.

Citizens affect politics.

Politics affect stability.

Stability affects the Morning Court.

The world should feel connected rather than random.

---

## Visible Change

Players should see change.

Do not hide every calculation behind numbers.

Examples:

- A rebuilt bridge appears on the road.
- A market becomes busier after trade improves.
- Fields become greener after irrigation research.
- Citizens gather near a new public building.
- Companions mention recent events during court.
- Cities visually evolve after major decisions.

Visible consequences make the kingdom believable.

---

## Kingdom Before Empire

Dream Palace is not about conquering the world first.

The player's first responsibility is protecting home.

Expansion should only feel meaningful after the kingdom becomes worth protecting.

Before the player rules more land, they should care about the land they already have.

---

# World Layers

The world consists of several connected layers.

Kingdom

↓

Regions

↓

Cities

↓

Districts

↓

Buildings

↓

People

Every major decision should eventually influence at least one of these layers.

The deeper the layer affected, the more personal the consequence should feel.

---

# The Kingdom

The kingdom is the emotional center of Dream Palace.

It is not only a political entity.

It is the player's home.

It contains:

- the throne
- the Morning Court
- the capital
- citizens
- farms
- workshops
- roads
- markets
- borderlands
- cultural memory

The kingdom should feel like a place the player can lose, protect, rebuild, and gradually understand.

---

# Regions

Regions give the kingdom texture.

Each region should have:

- a visual identity
- a major resource
- a social problem
- a local faction
- a gameplay purpose
- a possible emotional story

Regions should not exist only as resource nodes.

A forest is not only timber.

A border village is not only defense.

A river is not only transportation.

Each place should create stories.

---

# Cities and Settlements

Cities and settlements should visibly evolve.

A village can become safer.

A market can become richer.

A border town can become militarized.

A district can become abandoned.

A capital can become more alive.

Settlement growth should be shown through:

- architecture
- population density
- lighting
- citizen behavior
- companion comments
- new court topics

The player should feel that decisions leave marks on places.

---

# Economy

Money is only one resource.

The kingdom survives through:

- Grain
- Timber
- Stone
- Iron
- Gold
- Reputation
- Trust

Resources should feel physical.

Players should understand where resources come from and where they go.

A resource should not be only a number on a bar.

It should represent farmers, roads, warehouses, labor, weather, politics, and risk.

---

# Resource Flow

Resources should move through the world.

Example:

Farmers harvest grain.

↓

Grain moves through roads.

↓

Warehouses store it.

↓

MP reports supply.

↓

The player allocates it.

↓

Citizens, soldiers, or construction projects benefit.

When a link breaks, the kingdom should feel it.

A road collapse should affect delivery.

A bandit attack should affect security.

A bad harvest should affect prices and morale.

---

# Politics

Politics are driven by people.

Not percentages.

Ministers disagree.

Guilds negotiate.

Nobles have interests.

Citizens have expectations.

Local leaders remember promises.

Political change should emerge naturally through interaction and consequence.

The player should feel that ruling means managing trust, not only issuing commands.

---

# Factions

Every faction wants something.

Nobody exists to help the player for free.

Potential faction types include:

- Royal Court
- Merchant Guild
- Scholar Academy
- Military Command
- Religious Order
- Local Citizens
- Artisan Guilds
- Border Clans
- Foreign Kingdoms

Each faction should have:

- a desire
- a fear
- a resource
- a memory
- a relationship with the throne

Factions should remember how the player treated them.

---

# Technology

Technology should improve the kingdom.

It should not simply unlock bigger numbers.

Example:

Better irrigation

↓

Higher harvest

↓

Lower food prices

↓

Higher citizen happiness

↓

Greater stability

Technology should create stories.

When Kel or Shian proposes a technology, the player should understand how it changes daily life.

---

# Culture

Culture is not decoration.

Culture helps the kingdom understand itself.

Festivals, art, architecture, rituals, songs, clothing, food, and public spaces all influence how citizens feel about the throne.

Mika and Vincey should often express the cultural state of the kingdom.

A prosperous kingdom should feel warmer.

A frightened kingdom should feel quieter.

A divided kingdom should feel tense.

---

# Threats

Threats should emerge from world conditions whenever possible.

Bandits may rise when food is scarce.

Corruption may grow when oversight is weak.

Foreign powers may attack when borders are neglected.

Disasters may expose poor infrastructure.

Unrest may grow when citizens lose trust.

Threats are not only enemies.

They are symptoms of a living world.

---

# Living Events

Events should happen because of the world.

Not only because a timer reached zero.

Example:

Flood

↓

Food shortage

↓

Merchant prices increase

↓

Citizens complain

↓

Morning Court discussion

↓

Player decision

↓

Recovery or deeper crisis

One event should create another.

The best events feel inevitable in hindsight.

---

# Companion Relationship With The World

Companions are not isolated characters.

Each companion belongs to part of the kingdom.

| Companion | World Domain |
|-----------|--------------|
| MP | Treasury, taxation, resource allocation |
| Kel | Technology, construction, infrastructure |
| Tab | Administration, standards, accountability |
| CX | Investigation, intelligence, hidden threats |
| Mika | Culture, visual identity, morale |
| Vincey | Events, public mood, ceremonies |
| Shian | Planning, strategy, geography, logistics |

When their domain changes, their personality, dialogue, and opinions should also evolve.

If the treasury is healthy, MP should feel calmer.

If infrastructure fails, Kel and Shian should react.

If public morale collapses, Mika and Vincey should feel it.

If hidden threats grow, CX should become more urgent.

---

# The Morning Court as World Interface

The Morning Court is where the world speaks.

The player should not receive world updates as generic notifications.

The kingdom should report itself through people.

MP reports the treasury.

CX reports hidden threats.

Tab reports risks.

Kel reports construction and research.

Shian reports long-term planning.

Mika reports culture and morale.

Vincey reports public energy and events.

The Morning Court turns world simulation into character drama.

---

# Time

Time should make the world feel alive.

A day matters.

A season matters.

An absence matters.

The world should support:

- daily court reports
- seasonal harvests
- construction duration
- delayed consequences
- faction memory
- companion progress while the player is away

Time should not exist only to create waiting.

Time should create anticipation.

---

# Design Rules

The world should never feel static.

The world should never exist only for gameplay.

The world should continue changing without asking the player's permission.

World changes should be visible whenever possible.

World systems should create character conversations.

World problems should create leadership decisions.

---

# World Test

Ask:

If the player leaves for seven days, will they come back to a kingdom that feels different?

If the answer is no, the world is not alive enough.

Ask also:

Can the player see what changed?

Can companions explain why it changed?

Can the player respond through leadership?

If the answer is yes, the world belongs in Dream Palace.

---

# Final Reminder

The kingdom is not the stage.

The kingdom is one of the main characters.

---

> **A living kingdom remembers.**
